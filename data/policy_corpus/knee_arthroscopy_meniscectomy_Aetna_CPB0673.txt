## COVERAGE INDICATIONS — MEDICALLY NECESSARY

### Arthroscopic Knee Surgery (with or without Partial Meniscectomy or Meniscal Repair)

Arthroscopic knee surgery is considered medically necessary for persons presenting
with ALL of the following criteria:
1. Significant knee pain PLUS mechanical symptoms (e.g., locking, catching, giving way)
2. No more than mild osteoarthritis (Kellgren-Lawrence Grade 0, 1, or 2; OR modified
   Outerbridge Grade 0, 1, or 2)
3. Radiologic confirmation of the pathology:
   - X-ray for loose bodies
   - MRI for meniscal tears and/or loose bodies
4. Failure of conservative therapy (see definition below)

### Arthroscopic Partial Meniscectomy or Meniscal Repair for Traumatic Meniscal Tears

Arthroscopic partial meniscectomy or meniscal repair is considered medically necessary
when ALL of the following criteria are met:
1. Traumatic meniscal tear with significant symptoms
2. No more than mild osteoarthritis (Kellgren-Lawrence Grade 0, 1, or 2; OR modified
   Outerbridge Grade 0, 1, or 2)
3. MRI confirmation of the meniscal pathology
4. Failure of conservative therapy (see definition below)

Note: Conservative treatment may be waived if the member's knee is "locked" due to
a displaced bucket handle tear of the meniscus, as this is a surgical emergency that
cannot safely await a trial of conservative management.

### Arthroscopic Meniscal Repair for Meniscal Root Tears

Arthroscopic meniscal repair is considered medically necessary for the treatment of
medial and/or lateral meniscal root tears for members who have:
- Significant symptoms
- No more than mild osteoarthritis

## CONSERVATIVE THERAPY REQUIREMENT

History of unsuccessful conservative therapy is required before surgical authorization.
Conservative therapy is defined as non-surgical medical management including at least
six (6) weeks of formal, in-person physical therapy within the past year.

Requirements for conservative therapy documentation:
- Physical therapy must be in-person (not home-based or virtual PT)
- PT course must be confirmed by actual PT notes OR documentation in the member's
  claims history
- Documentation should include dates, frequency, and type of treatment received,
  and the clinical response (or lack thereof)

Exception: Conservative treatment requirement may be waived if the patient's knee is
"locked" due to a displaced bucket handle tear of the meniscus, representing a
mechanical block to range of motion requiring urgent intervention.

## IMAGING REQUIREMENTS

Prior authorization for knee arthroscopy requires radiologically proven pathology:
- MRI of the knee confirming meniscal tear, meniscal root tear, or loose body
- Arthroscopy is only considered medically necessary and covered for persons with
  radiologically proven pathology meeting the above criteria
- The MRI must confirm the specific pathology for which surgery is planned

## NON-COVERED INDICATIONS (NOT MEDICALLY NECESSARY)

### Arthroscopic Debridement for Osteoarthritis

Arthroscopic debridement is NOT medically necessary for persons with osteoarthritis
of the knee presenting with:
- Knee pain only (without mechanical symptoms), OR
- Severe osteoarthritis (Outerbridge classification III or IV)

Rationale: Clinical evidence does not demonstrate that arthroscopic debridement or
lavage provides lasting benefit over sham surgery or conservative management for
patients with osteoarthritis-related knee pain without a defined mechanical lesion.

### Meniscectomy for Meniscal Root Tears (Open or Arthroscopic)

Meniscectomy (arthroscopic or open; total or partial) for the treatment of medial or
lateral meniscal root tears is considered experimental and investigational. Meniscal
repair is the preferred approach when a root tear is the primary diagnosis.

### Diagnostic Arthroscopy

Diagnostic knee arthroscopy (CPT code 29870) is generally NOT considered medically
necessary and is not covered. Knee arthroscopy is only considered medically necessary
and covered for persons with radiologically proven pathology that meets the criteria
above. The diagnosis should be established by non-invasive imaging (MRI) prior to
surgical planning.

## CLINICAL BACKGROUND

### Meniscal Tears and Mechanical Symptoms

The meniscus serves as a shock absorber in the knee joint and plays a critical role
in load distribution and joint stability. Traumatic meniscal tears (as distinct from
degenerative tears associated with osteoarthritis) typically occur in younger patients
through twisting or rotational injury to the knee.

Mechanical symptoms are a key indicator for surgical candidacy. These include:
- Locking (inability to fully extend the knee, often from a displaced meniscal fragment)
- Catching or clicking with motion
- Giving way (sudden loss of stability)
- Pain with weight-bearing or twisting movements

### Osteoarthritis Grading

The Kellgren-Lawrence (KL) grading system is used to classify severity of knee
osteoarthritis on plain radiographs:
- Grade 0: No features of osteoarthritis
- Grade 1: Doubtful narrowing of joint space; possible osteophytic lipping (mild)
- Grade 2: Possible narrowing of joint space; definite osteophytes (mild)
- Grade 3: Definite narrowing of joint space, moderate multiple osteophytes (moderate)
- Grade 4: Marked narrowing of joint space, large osteophytes, severe sclerosis (severe)

Arthroscopy is NOT expected to provide benefit in the setting of moderate-to-severe
osteoarthritis (KL Grade 3-4), as the procedure cannot address the underlying
degenerative joint changes driving symptoms in these patients.

### Conservative Management for Knee Injuries

Conservative management for meniscal tears and knee pain includes:
- Formal physical therapy (at least 6 weeks, in-person): strengthening, range of motion,
  proprioceptive training
- NSAIDs and analgesics for pain management
- Activity modification and temporary reduction in weight-bearing activities
- Corticosteroid injections for pain and inflammation
- Ice, compression, and elevation during acute phase
