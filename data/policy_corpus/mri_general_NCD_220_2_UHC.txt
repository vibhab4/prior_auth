## OVERVIEW

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is a non-invasive method of graphically representing
the distribution of water and other hydrogen-rich molecules in the human body. Unlike
conventional radiographs or computed tomography (CT) scans, MRI is capable of producing
images by several techniques. The basic elements by which MRI produces an image are the
density of hydrogen nuclei in the object being examined, their motion, and the relaxation
times.

Among the advantages of MRI are the absence of ionizing radiation and the ability to
achieve high levels of tissue contrast resolution without injected iodinated radiological
contrast agents. The use of MRI for soft tissue structures for the purpose of detecting
disruptive, neoplastic, degenerative, or inflammatory lesions has become established in
medical practice.

Coverage is limited to MRI units that have received FDA premarket approval, and such
units must be operated within the parameters specified by the approval. In addition,
services must be reasonable and necessary for the diagnosis or treatment of the specific
patient involved.

## NATIONALLY COVERED MRI INDICATIONS

### 1. MRI (Structural Imaging)

Although several uses of MRI are still considered investigational and some are clearly
contraindicated, MRI is considered medically efficacious for a number of uses. The
following are general guidelines or examples of what may be considered covered, rather
than a restrictive list of specific covered indications:

a) Head, Central Nervous System, and Spine (Effective November 22, 1985):
   MRI is useful in examining the head, central nervous system, and spine. Multiple
   sclerosis can be diagnosed with MRI and the contents of the posterior fossa are
   visible. The inherent tissue contrast resolution of MRI makes it an appropriate
   standard diagnostic modality for general neuroradiology.

b) Mediastinal/Retroperitoneal Masses and Soft Tissue (Effective November 22, 1985):
   MRI can assist in the differential diagnosis of mediastinal and retroperitoneal
   masses, including abnormalities of the large vessels such as aneurysms and dissection.
   When a clinical need exists to visualize parenchyma of solid organs to detect anatomic
   disruption or neoplasia, this can be accomplished in the liver, urogenital system,
   adrenals, and pelvic organs without the use of radiological contrast materials. When
   MRI is considered reasonable and necessary, the use of paramagnetic contrast materials
   may be covered as part of the study. MRI may also be used to detect and stage pelvic
   and retroperitoneal neoplasms and to evaluate disorders of cancellous bone and soft
   tissues. It may also be used in the detection of pericardial thickening. Primary and
   secondary bone neoplasm and aseptic necrosis can be detected at an early stage with
   MRI. Patients with metallic prostheses, especially of the hip, can be imaged in order
   to detect the early stages of infection of the bone to which the prosthesis is attached.

c) Cardiac and Respiratory Gating (Effective March 4, 1991):
   MRI with gating devices and surface coils, and gating devices that eliminate distorted
   images caused by cardiac and respiratory movement cycles, are now considered state of
   the art techniques and may be covered. Surface and other specialty coils may also be
   covered, as they are used routinely for high resolution imaging where small limited
   regions of the body are studied.

d) Disc Disease (Effective March 22, 1994):
   MRI may also be covered to diagnose disc disease without regard to whether radiological
   imaging has been tried first to diagnose the problem.

### 2. MRA (Magnetic Resonance Angiography — MRI for Blood Flow)

Currently covered indications include using MRA for specific conditions to evaluate flow
in internal carotid vessels of the head and neck, peripheral arteries of lower
extremities, abdomen and pelvis, and the chest. Coverage is limited to MRI units with
FDA premarket approval, and such units must be operated within parameters specified by
the approval.

#### a) Head and Neck MRA (Effective April 15, 2003)
All of the following criteria must apply:
- MRA is used to evaluate the carotid arteries, the circle of Willis, the anterior,
  middle or posterior cerebral arteries, the vertebral or basilar arteries, or the
  venous sinuses
- MRA is performed on patients with conditions of the head and neck for which surgery
  is anticipated and may be found to be appropriate based on the MRA (conditions
  include, but are not limited to: tumor, aneurysms, vascular malformations, vascular
  occlusion or thrombosis)
- MRA and conventional angiography (CA) are not expected to be performed on the same
  patient for diagnostic purposes prior to the application of anticipated therapy;
  only one of these tests will be covered routinely unless the physician can demonstrate
  the medical need to perform both

#### b) Peripheral Arteries of Lower Extremities MRA (Effective April 15, 2003)
Effective for determining the presence and extent of peripheral vascular disease in
lower extremities. Medicare will cover either MRA or CA to evaluate peripheral arteries
of the lower extremities; both may be useful in some cases (e.g., when CA is
inconclusive or when viable runoff vessel cannot be identified for bypass planning).

#### c) Abdomen and Pelvis MRA
- Pre-operative evaluation of patients undergoing elective Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm
  (AAA) repair (Effective July 1, 1999)
- Imaging of the Renal Arteries and the Aortoiliac Arteries in the Absence of AAA or
  Aortic Dissection (Effective July 1, 2003): MRA should be obtained when physician
  history, physical examination, and standard assessment tools provide insufficient
  information for patient management, and obtaining an MRA has a high probability of
  positively affecting patient management.

#### d) Chest MRA
- Diagnosis of Pulmonary Embolism: Medicare will cover MRA of the chest for diagnosing
  a suspected pulmonary embolism when it is contraindicated for the patient to receive
  intravascular iodinated contrast material
- Evaluation of Thoracic Aortic Dissection and Aneurysm: MRA of the chest has a high
  level of diagnostic accuracy for pre-operative and post-operative evaluation; may be
  used as an alternative to transesophageal echocardiography and CT

#### e) Cardiac MRA for Velocity Flow Mapping
Cardiac velocity flow mapping is considered medically reasonable and necessary for
quantitative assessment of structural defects including:
- Magnitude of cardiac shunt fractions in patients with atrial septal defect,
  ventricular septal defect, or patent ductus arteriosus
- Valvular regurgitation fractions in patients with valvular regurgitation or
  insufficiency
- Grading of valvular, subvalvular, or supravalvular stenosis
- Evaluation of differential flow of the pulmonary arteries in Tetrology of Fallot

## NATIONALLY NON-COVERED INDICATIONS

### MRI Contraindications

The MRI is NOT covered when the following patient-specific contraindications are
present:
- Patients with cardiac pacemakers or with metallic clips on vascular aneurysms

Exceptions:
- Effective July 7, 2011: The contraindications will not apply to pacemakers when
  used according to the FDA-approved labeling in an MRI environment
- Effective February 24, 2011: CMS believes that the evidence is promising (although
  not yet convincing) that MRI will improve patient health outcomes if certain
  safeguards are in place to ensure that the exposure of the device to an MRI
  environment adversely affects neither the interpretation of the MRI result nor the
  proper functioning of the implanted cardiac device

### Non-Covered MRI Uses

CMS has determined that MRI of cortical bone and calcifications, and procedures
involving spatial resolution of bone and calcifications, are NOT considered reasonable
and necessary indications within the meaning of section 1862(a)(1)(A) of the Act,
and are therefore non-covered.

### Non-Covered MRA Uses

The following MRA services are considered NOT medically reasonable and necessary:
- MRA of the spinal canal and contents
- MRA of the upper Extremities

## COVERAGE FOR UNLISTED INDICATIONS

Effective June 3, 2010, all other uses of MRI or MRA for which CMS has not
specifically indicated national coverage or national non-coverage continue to be
eligible for coverage through individual local contractor discretion. For unspecified
body regions or uses not addressed by this NCD, coverage decisions are made by the
patient's Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC) via Local Coverage Determinations
(LCDs).

- Claims with ICD-9 diagnosis code V45.01 (pacemaker) will be denied unless modifier
  KX is also present on the line, confirming that requirements of the medical policy
  have been met (effective July 7, 2011)
- Modifier KX: Requirements specified in the medical policy have been met
- Medicare will allow coverage of MRI for beneficiaries with implanted PMs or
  cardioverter defibrillators (ICDs) for use in an MRI environment in a
  Medicare-approved clinical study (effective February 24, 2011)
